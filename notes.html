2.3 Nearest points map and supporting hyperplane

2.3.1 Lemma

Let \(K\subset \R^n\) be closed and convex. Then for every \(x\in \R^n\), there is unique point \(x^1 \in K\) such that \[\Vert x - x^1\Vert = \inf\Vert x - y\Vert = d(x, K)\]

2.3.2 Proof

We can find a sequence of points \((y_n)\) in \(M\) such that the distance from \(x\) is less than \(1/n\). Now, the sequence is Cauchy. Since, \(\R^n\) is complete, it has to converge, and since \(K\) is closed, we are done. 4

Uniqueness: Given \(X\), if there are two points \(x'\) and \(x''\) such that the distances from \(x\) from these two points are the same. In the plane \(x, x', x''\), the triangle \(\Delta x x' x''\) exists. But then a perpendicular to the side \(x'x''\) would be smaller than the distance to \(x'\) or \(x''\). This is a contradiction. Hence the points have to be unique. (Here the convexity of the set is used.)

2.3.3 Definition of nearest point map

Given \(K\subset \R^n\) be a closed convex set. Then \(p_k \mathbb \R^n \rightarrow K\) is the nearest points map. (This is defined using the last lemma.)

If \(x\in K\), then \(p_k(x) = x\). \(p_K\) is surjective. Usually it is not injective, if \(K = \R^n\), then it is injective.
